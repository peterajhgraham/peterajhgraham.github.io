This comprehensive analysis examines obesity rates across all 50 states, Washington D.C., and Puerto Rico, highlighting the significant variations between different regions and states. The analysis is based on data from Data.gov - a repository of government data of all types (local, state, and federal).

Through detailed visualizations, we explore the geographic distribution of obesity, compare states with the highest and lowest rates, and analyze the overall distribution pattern. These insights help understand the complex factors contributing to obesity rates across the United States.

All analysis, charts, data narrative, and website design were created by Peter Graham.

Key Findings

Analysis reveals distinct regional patterns in obesity rates, with certain regions showing significantly higher prevalence than others. There appears to be a correlation between political leaning and obesity rates, with more liberal states generally showing lower obesity rates compared to conservative counterparts.

Research Questions

This analysis sought to answer the following questions:

  • How do obesity rates vary geographically across the United States?
  • What patterns emerge when comparing obesity rates between states?
  • Is there a correlation between political leanings and obesity rates?
  • What is the national average obesity rate and how do states compare to this benchmark?

The geographic distribution of obesity rates reveals clear regional patterns across the United States. As shown in the visualization, states are arranged by obesity rate within their respective regions (West, South, Northeast, and Midwest), with colors ranging from light yellow (lowest rates) to dark purple (highest rates).

This regional analysis demonstrates that the West and Northeast regions generally have lower obesity rates, while the South and Midwest tend to have higher rates. Colorado consistently shows the lowest obesity rate nationwide at approximately 20%, while Louisiana has among the highest at approximately 36%.

The visualization also highlights that within each region, there can be significant variations in obesity rates. For example, while many Southern states have high obesity rates (appearing as dark purple), some states in the South have moderate rates. Similarly, the Midwest shows a range of obesity prevalence across states.

Distribution Analysis

Figure 3: Histogram showing the distribution of obesity rates across states with density curve and mean indicator

This analysis examines the distribution of obesity rates across all states and territories, revealing a national average of 29.3%. As shown in the histogram with density curve, the distribution of obesity rates across states ranges from approximately 15% to 40%.

The red vertical dashed line indicates the mean obesity rate of 29.3%, with the density curve showing that a significant number of states have obesity rates exceeding this threshold. The histogram shows a bimodal distribution with peaks around 25-27% and 29-31%, suggesting two potential clusters of states.

The distribution shows a slight rightward skew, indicating that while there are several states with relatively low obesity rates, many states cluster around or above the national average. This analysis serves as a call to awareness regarding obesity in America, highlighting that the majority of states have obesity rates of 25% or higher.

The disparity between the highest and lowest state obesity rates - a difference of approximately 20 percentage points - highlights the significant regional variations in this public health concern and suggests the influence of diverse factors including socioeconomic conditions, food environments/deserts, physical activity opportunities, and policy approaches.

Methodology

This analysis was conducted using data from the National Obesity By State dataset available on Data.gov. The methodology involved the following steps:

Data Collection

The dataset was obtained from the official Data.gov repository, which includes comprehensive obesity statistics for all 50 states, Washington D.C., and Puerto Rico.

Data Cleaning and Preparation

The raw data was cleaned to ensure consistency and accuracy. Missing values were addressed, and data formats were standardized for analysis.

Analysis Techniques

Statistical analysis was performed using R to calculate descriptive statistics, identify patterns, and visualize the data. Geographic analysis was conducted to map obesity rates across regions.

Visualization Creation

Data visualizations were created using R's plotting capabilities, with emphasis on clarity and informative representation of the findings.

Dataset Source

This analysis uses data from the National Obesity By State dataset available on Data.gov. The dataset provides comprehensive obesity statistics for all U.S. states and territories, offering a robust foundation for analyzing geographic and demographic patterns in obesity prevalence.

Access the complete dataset here

For those interested in conducting their own analysis, the dataset includes variables such as state name, obesity rates, confidence intervals, and associated metadata. This publicly available resource enables transparent verification of the findings presented in this analysis.
